# Glossary of Terms

## A

**Action**: In ROS 2, a goal-oriented communication pattern that provides feedback during execution and can be preempted. Used for long-running tasks with intermediate results.

**Actuator**: A mechanical device for moving or controlling a mechanism or system. In robotics, actuators convert energy (often electrical) into physical motion.

**APA Format**: American Psychological Association style for citing references, commonly used in academic writing. In this textbook, all citations follow APA 7th edition format.

**Articulation**: A joint or connection between parts of a robot that allows for movement. In Isaac Sim, articulations refer to the joints that connect different parts of a robot.

## B

**Behavior Tree**: A hierarchical structure used in robotics and AI for organizing and controlling complex behaviors. It provides a modular and reusable approach to task planning and execution.

**Bipedal Locomotion**: The act of walking on two legs, a key challenge in humanoid robotics that requires sophisticated balance and control algorithms.

**Body Schema**: A representation of the robot's physical structure, including the spatial relationships between its parts, essential for coordinated movement.

## C

**Citation**: A reference to a published source used to support technical claims. This textbook requires all claims to be verifiable with credible sources using APA format.

**Computer Vision**: A field of artificial intelligence that trains computers to interpret and understand the visual world. In robotics, it enables robots to identify objects and navigate environments.

**Control Theory**: The engineering discipline that deals with the behavior of dynamical systems with inputs, and how their behavior is modified by feedback.

**Coordinate Frame**: A system for specifying positions and orientations in space. In robotics, coordinate frames are essential for navigation and manipulation tasks.

**Cognitive Planning**: The process of creating sequences of actions to achieve goals, often using LLMs to translate high-level commands into specific robot behaviors.

## D

**DDS (Data Distribution Service)**: The middleware standard that ROS 2 uses for communication, providing a publish-subscribe pattern for distributed systems.

**Degrees of Freedom (DOF)**: The number of independent movements a mechanical system can make. For a humanoid robot, this includes movements of arms, legs, torso, and head.

**Differential Drive**: A type of wheeled robot locomotion using two independently driven wheels on either side of the robot body.

**Docusaurus**: A static site generator used for creating documentation websites, including this textbook.

**Dynamics**: The study of forces and torques and their effect on motion, essential for understanding robot movement and control.

## E

**Embodied Intelligence**: AI systems that operate in physical reality, subject to the laws of physics and real-world constraints, as opposed to purely digital AI systems.

**End Effector**: The device at the end of a robotic arm designed to interact with the environment. For humanoid robots, this is typically a hand for manipulation tasks.

**Epipolar Geometry**: The geometry of stereo vision, describing the relationship between points in two images of the same scene.

## F

**Forward Kinematics**: The use of joint angles to compute the position and orientation of the end effector in a robotic system.

**Fused Multi-Sense**: A perception approach that combines data from multiple sensors to create a more complete understanding of the environment.

## G

**Gazebo**: A physics-based simulation environment that enables realistic robot simulation with accurate physics, sensors, and environments.

**Gimbal Lock**: A loss of one degree of freedom in a three-dimensional, three-gimbal mechanism that occurs when the axes of two of the three gimbals are driven into a parallel configuration.

**GPU (Graphics Processing Unit)**: A specialized electronic circuit designed to rapidly manipulate and alter memory to accelerate the creation of images in a frame buffer intended for output to a display device.

## H

**Hardware-in-the-Loop (HIL)**: A testing technique that involves physical hardware components in a simulated environment.

**HRI (Human-Robot Interaction)**: The study of interactions between humans and robots, particularly important for humanoid systems designed to work with people.

**Humanoid Robot**: A robot with a human-like body structure, typically including legs for locomotion, arms for manipulation, and a head with sensors.

## I

**Isaac Sim**: NVIDIA's robotics simulation environment built on Omniverse, providing photorealistic simulation and synthetic data generation capabilities.

**Isaac ROS**: NVIDIA's collection of hardware-accelerated perception and navigation packages that run on ROS 2.

**IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit)**: A device that measures and reports a body's specific force, angular rate, and sometimes the magnetic field surrounding the body.

**Inverse Kinematics**: The mathematical process of determining the joint angles required to place the end effector at a desired position and orientation.

## J

**Joint**: A connection between two or more links in a robotic system that allows for relative motion between them.

**Jacobian Matrix**: A matrix of partial derivatives that describes the relationship between joint velocities and end-effector velocities in robotics.

## K

**Kinematics**: The study of motion without considering the forces that cause it, essential for understanding robot movement.

**Kinematic Chain**: An assembly of rigid bodies connected by joints that transmit motion, fundamental to robot design.

## L

**LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging)**: A remote sensing method that uses light in the form of a pulsed laser to measure distances to objects, creating precise, three-dimensional information about shape and surface characteristics.

**LLM (Large Language Model)**: A type of artificial intelligence model that can understand and generate human language, used in this textbook for cognitive planning.

**Locomotion**: The ability to move from one place to another, a fundamental capability for mobile robots including bipedal locomotion for humanoid systems.

**Logistics**: The detailed coordination of a complex operation involving many people, facilities, or supplies, relevant for multi-robot systems.

## M

**Middleware**: Software that provides common services and capabilities to applications beyond what's offered by the operating system, such as ROS 2 for robotics.

**Mobile Robot**: A robot that can move around in its environment using actuated wheels, tracks, or legs.

**Morphological Computation**: The idea that the physical form of a robot can contribute to intelligent behavior, reducing the computational burden on the control system.

## N

**Nav2**: The navigation stack for mobile robots in ROS 2, providing path planning, obstacle avoidance, and localization capabilities.

**Node**: In ROS 2, a process that performs computation. Nodes are the fundamental building blocks of ROS 2 applications.

**Non-Holonomic**: A system with constraints that cannot be integrated to form a set of constraints on only the coordinates, such as wheeled robots that cannot move sideways.

## O

**Odometry**: The use of data from motion sensors to estimate change in position over time, crucial for robot navigation.

**Omnidirectional**: The ability to move in any direction, in contrast to non-holonomic systems with motion constraints.

**OpenCV**: An open-source computer vision and machine learning software library used extensively in robotics applications.

**Operational Space**: The space in which the robot's end effector operates, typically described in Cartesian coordinates.

## P

**Path Planning**: The computational problem of finding a valid sequence of configurations to move an object from a source to a destination.

**Perception**: The ability of a robot to interpret sensory information from its environment, including vision, touch, and other modalities.

**PID Controller**: A control loop mechanism employing feedback that is widely used in industrial control systems and robotics.

**Point Cloud**: A set of data points in space, typically representing the external surface of an object, often generated by 3D scanners or LiDAR.

**Pose**: The position and orientation of a robot or object in space, typically described by position coordinates and rotation angles.

## R

**ROS (Robot Operating System)**: A flexible framework for writing robot software, providing services designed for a heterogeneous computer cluster.

**ROS 2**: The second generation of the Robot Operating System, featuring improved real-time capabilities, security, and multi-robot support.

**RRT (Rapidly-exploring Random Tree)**: A path planning algorithm that builds a space-filling tree by randomly sampling the search space and extending the tree at each step.

**Rigid Body**: An object that maintains its shape and size regardless of the forces acting upon it, a fundamental concept in robot dynamics.

**Robotics Middleware**: Software that provides common services and capabilities to robot applications, such as communication, device abstraction, and concurrency management.

**ROS Package**: A container for organizing ROS functionality, including source code, configuration files, and documentation.

## S

**Sensor Fusion**: The process of combining data from multiple sensors to achieve better accuracy and reliability than could be achieved by using a single sensor.

**SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping)**: The computational problem of constructing or updating a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously keeping track of an agent's location within it.

**Simulation**: The imitation of the operation of a real-world process or system over time, essential for robotics development and testing.

**Stability**: The ability of a robot to maintain balance and continue operation despite disturbances, crucial for humanoid locomotion.

**Stereo Vision**: The process of extracting 3D information from 2D images captured by two cameras positioned at different angles.

**System Integration**: The process of bringing together the component subsystems into one system and ensuring that the system performs as a whole.

## T

**Topic**: In ROS 2, a named bus over which nodes exchange messages using a publish-subscribe communication pattern.

**Trajectory**: A path that an object follows through space as a function of time, important for robot motion planning.

**Twist Message**: In ROS, a message type that represents the velocity of a robot in free space, typically including linear and angular velocities.

## U

**URDF (Unified Robot Description Format)**: An XML format for representing a robot model, including physical and visual properties, used extensively in ROS.

**Unity**: A real-time 3D development platform used for high-fidelity visualization and human-robot interaction scenarios in this textbook.

**Unstructured Environment**: An environment without predefined paths or markers, requiring robots to navigate using perception and mapping.

## V

**VLA (Vision-Language-Action)**: Systems that integrate visual perception, language understanding, and physical action, representing the integration of multiple AI modalities.

**VSLAM (Visual Simultaneous Localization and Mapping)**: SLAM using visual sensors such as cameras instead of or in addition to other sensor types.

**Vision System**: The combination of hardware and software used by a robot to interpret visual information from its environment.

## W

**Whisper**: An automatic speech recognition (ASR) system developed by OpenAI, used in this textbook for voice command recognition.

**Whole-Body Control**: A control approach that considers the entire robot body when generating motion, important for humanoid robots with many degrees of freedom.

**Workspace**: The volume of space that a robot manipulator can reach, determined by the robot's kinematic structure.

## X, Y, Z

**YAML (YAML Ain't Markup Language)**: A human-readable data serialization format used in ROS for configuration files.

**Zero Moment Point (ZMP)**: A criterion for static and dynamic stability of legged robots, particularly important for humanoid walking algorithms.

**ZMP (Zero Moment Point)**: A dynamic balance criterion used in humanoid robotics to ensure stable walking patterns.
